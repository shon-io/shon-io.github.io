The Walmart Effect, Everywhere

Against Efficiency — Post 1

When was the last time you bought something from a person you knew?

Not a transaction. Not a fulfillment. Not a delivery window. A purchase from a human being who lived near you, knew your name, and had a stake in whether you came back.

Think hard. It might have been longer ago than you’d like to admit.

That’s no accident. It’s the predictable, measurable, documented result of forty years of economic policy, corporate strategy, and consumer behavior that prioritized one thing above all others: efficiency. Lower prices. Faster delivery. Frictionless transactions. And in pursuit of that efficiency, we traded away something we didn’t know we were selling: the local economic fabric that held communities together, kept wealth circulating close to home, and gave individuals — merchants, farmers, makers, and just plain people — a fighting chance.

This is the first in a series of posts about what we traded, why we traded it, and what it would take to get it back. I’m not going to be polite about it because the situation doesn’t call for politeness.

The Pattern Hiding in Plain Sight

Walk down the main street of almost any American town and you’ll see the same thing: a Dollar General. A chain pharmacy. A fast food cluster. Maybe a regional grocery that absorbed the local one a decade ago. The independent hardware store is gone. The family-owned clothing shop is gone. The local bank branch — the one where the manager knew your business — is gone.

Don’t misunderstand - this isn’t nostalgic.

The retail sector tells the story plainly. Walmart alone accounts for roughly a quarter of all grocery sales in the United States. Amazon captures nearly forty cents of every e-commerce dollar spent. In county after county across the country, a single company controls the majority of grocery retail, the majority of pharmacy retail, the majority of what used to be called the general store. The names change by sector, but the pattern doesn’t.

Agriculture is worse. Four companies process roughly 80% of American beef. A similar handful control pork, poultry, and grain. The independent farmer — the one who owned equipment, made decisions, and kept profit in the local economy — has been systematically replaced by a contract grower: someone who owns the land and the debt, but not the animals, not the feed, and not the price they’ll be paid at the end of the season. They bear the risk. Someone else captures the reward.

Technology has perfected the model. A handful of platforms now intermediate nearly every economic transaction that moves through a screen. Want to sell handmade goods? Etsy takes a cut and owns your customer relationship. Want to get your restaurant’s food to someone across town? DoorDash will do it for thirty percent of every order, plus your customer’s data, plus the right to advertise your competitors back to your own diners. Want to reach an audience for your writing, your music, your craft? The platform decides who sees it, on what terms, and reserves the right to change those terms whenever it likes.

Instead of a collection of isolated industries making independent choices, it’s a single phenomenon wearing different clothes.

Efficiency Did This. Deliberately.

The standard defense of consolidation goes like this: companies got big because consumers chose them. Walmart is cheaper. Amazon is faster. DoorDash is more convenient. Nobody was forced to shop there. The market spoke.

This defense is not entirely wrong which is what makes it so dangerous.

Consolidation did deliver real value, at least initially. Prices fell, selection expanded, logistics improved in ways that genuinely benefited people with limited time and limited money. Pretending otherwise loses the argument before it starts.

But efficiency is not a value-neutral force. It optimizes for what you measure, and what got measured was price, speed, and volume. What didn’t get measured was community wealth and resilience: the social infrastructure of a functioning local economy; the dignity of owning your own tools and your own customer relationships; the capacity of a town to survive when the supply chain breaks.

We got a lesson in that last one not long ago. When COVID-19 disrupted global supply chains in 2020, the fragility of a maximally efficient, maximally consolidated economy became impossible to ignore. Shelves went empty not because there was no food — there was food — but because the system had been optimized so thoroughly for normal conditions that it had no capacity to absorb an abnormal one. The independent butcher, the local farm stand, the regional food network - the inefficient alternatives — turned out to be more resilient than the efficient ones. But we had spent decades eliminating them.

Economic efficiency, it turns out, is just another way to say “fragility.”

The Replacement

What consolidation actually does, beneath the price tags and the convenience, is replace people.

It replaces the merchant with a platform, and the farmer with a contract grower. It replaces the mechanic with a terms-of-service clause that says you can’t repair what you own, and the local banker with an algorithm that doesn’t know your name. It replaces the community with a customer base, and the neighbor with a user.

Then, having replaced the people, it extracts from them. Every transaction carries a toll. Every platform collects a tax. Every proprietary system, every piece of farm equipment that requires a manufacturer’s technician to diagnose, every marketplace that owns your reviews and your customer history, every software subscription that holds your own data hostage, is a recurring transfer of wealth from the individual to the consolidated entity.

This is not capitalism working as intended. But it is capitalism working as it tends to when left entirely to its own devices: toward monopoly, toward extraction, toward the slow erosion of individual agency in favor of institutional control. Left and right can argue about what caused it and who’s to blame, but they can agree on what it looks like from the bottom - it looks like less: less independence, less ownership, less say, less money staying in the community where it was earned.

What This Series Is About

I’m not here to tell you to feel bad about shopping on Amazon or eating at a chain restaurant. Moral shaming is not a strategy, and it doesn’t work anyway.

I am here to argue that the consolidation of economic power into fewer and fewer hands — in retail, agriculture, technology, manufacturing, and beyond — is one of the defining problems of our time, that it has a specific shape and a specific logic, and that there are specific, practical alternatives that don’t require a political revolution or a vow of consumer poverty.

Those alternatives are local. They are distributed. They involve people making things, sharing the designs for those things, and building the economic and technological infrastructure to support a different kind of economy — one that keeps wealth close to home, returns ownership to individuals, and builds the kind of resilience that efficiency, by definition, cannot.

This isn’t nostalgia for a past that wasn’t as good as we remember, but an argument for a future that’s better than the one we’re currently building.

We’ve been replaced. That doesn’t mean we have to stay replaced.
